Genre alphabetical

Acrostic: The first letters of each line vertically form into a word.
Ballad: A narrative poem that made to be sung.
Blackout Poetry: Poems made by erasing words from an existing text.
Concrete poetry: The text and space of the poem is used to form an image.
Didactic poetry: Poems with a clear moral message or purpose to instruct.
Dirge: A short hymn or song about grief or lamentation.
Elegy: Poems that lament about death but ends in consolation.
Epic: Long narrative poems about heroes on mythical or histotic adventures.
Free Verse: Follows the natural pattern of speech instead of rhyming schemes.
Haiku: Poem style that is made of 3 unrhymed lines of 5, 7, then 5 syllables.
Hymn: A religous poem of praise, often to be sung.
Limerick: Whimiscal short poems of 5 lines in the AABBA rhyming scheme.
Lullaby: A bedtime song or chant used to put children to sleep.
Nursery Rhyme: A traditional children's poem or song.
Occasional poem: Poems written to describe an event or to be performed during it.
Ode: A formal or celebratory poem for a person, place, thing, or idea.
Pantoum: A style which is in sets of 4, with the 2nd and 4th lines repeating as next's 1st and 3rd lines.
Riddle: A question posed a problem to be solved or guessed.
Spoken word: Poetry intended to be performed instead of read.
Syllabic verse: The poem's rythmn comes from the number of syllables per line.
Terza rima: This style is made of 3-lined segments with the rhyming scheme of ABA BCB DED EFE, and so on.
